Teenage pregnancy

Teenage pregnancy is a global phenomenon with clearly known causes and serious health, social, and economic consequences. It comes at a significant cost to the mother and the new born child, to their family and the wider society, and increasing inequality. Teenage pregnancy has also been central to the global health and the development plans of the current sustainable development goals (SDGs), specifically, Goal 3, Target 3.7, which seeks to guarantee global access to reproductive health care facilities. Despite several measures taken to tackle the issue of teenage pregnancy by international communities and governments, the phenomenon persists as a global public health concern. However, the prevalence of teenage pregnancy varies between and within countries. Sub-Saharan Africa has among the highest prevalence of teenage pregnancy with Nigeria standing out with a 104% adolescent fertility rate in comparison to Asia’s 40.1% (UNDSEA). The high teenage pregnancy rate in Nigeria and other African countries portends danger to the actualization of the Sustainable Development Goal 5 (i.e., achieve gender equality and empower all women and girls) by 2030. According to The World Health Organisation (WHO), teenage or adolescent pregnancy is defined as those in young women between ages 10 and 19. Young women aged 15 to 19 are classified as adolescents, while girls between 10 and 14 are called young adolescents. The pregnancy rate counts pregnancies ending in births and abortions. About 400,000 unplanned births occur annually in Nigeria and half of these births are to teenage girls between the ages of 15 and 19 years.

Prevalence of Teenage Pregnancy

In a report by the United Nations Population Fund, 20,000 girls under age 18 give birth daily in developing countries like Nigeria. This amounts to 7.3 million births per year. About 3 million girls in this age bracket resort to unsafe abortions, risking their lives and health. Studies suggest that there are geographical differences in teenage pregnancy in Nigeria. While every three teenage girls in Northern Nigeria get pregnant, the corresponding figure is one out of ten girls in the South. According to a 2018 survey by Africa Check, female teenagers in Nigeria’s north-west geopolitical region were almost five times (29%) as likely to have had children as their peers in Nigeria’s south-west region (6%). For instance, 166 out of every 1000 teenage girls in Bauchi state give birth to children as against a lower birth rate of 22 out of every thousand in Lagos state (UNICEF, NICS 2021/22 Report). However, the pregnancy rate is higher in rural areas (from 29% in 2003 to 32% in 2018) and dips among girls in urban areas, (from 17% in 2003 to 8% in 2018). Research attributes this to increasing levels of education. Also, teenage girls with lower levels of education, lower-income households and living in rural areas are more likely to experience teenage pregnancy compared with those from high socioeconomic status (SES) backgrounds. Further, teenage pregnancy is also prevalent where access to education, information, and healthcare services are limited, due to higher barriers to obtaining and using contraceptives and other preventive measures.
